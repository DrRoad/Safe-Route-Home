Inspiration

We wanted to create a web based application that was helpful and meaningful. We identified that 50% of women said they did not feel safe walking home at night. Thus, we wanted to create an application that would allow users to get from point A to point safely in the city of Boston

Yet the definition of "safety" can vary greatly, and the current safety applications available often times are inherently racist by ignoring POC communities.

As a result, we created an application that allows you to select crimes you want to avoid based on your level of comfort. We researched the following statistics and found that saftey could be applicable in several ways

  • 12% of pedestrian injuries are caused by being hit by a car
  • 65% of women experience street harassment
  • 43% experience physical assault

User Research

In order to design a useful app, we talked to more than ten people to get a sense of what they avoid when walking around Boston, what they wish to avoid, and how they would prioritize it. Some themes included:

Social Interactions

"I want to avoid trash on the ground, puddles, mud, broken pavement, homeless/pan handlers (I feel bad but it's true)" - Wellesley College Alum, female, POC

"I want to avoid sexual harrassment or catcalling." - Olin College senior, female, POC

"So I tend to avoid eye contact with men who are standing around as I'm walking on the street." - Olin College senior, female, POC
Construction
"I avoid super-touristy areas and construction sites" - Babson College senior, male, POC

"Construction!" - Wellesley College senior, female, white
Vehicles
"Ooh I avoid cars at Wellesley by stopping a few feet before crossing the road so that they don't wait for me to pass." - Wellesley College senior, female, POC

"Highways and hills." - Olin College alum, male, POC

Prioritization of Crime Data

Alongside gathering insights from students, we also came across Boston crime data, which we thought may contain data that could help determine a preferred route for our users. We asked the same students if the data would be useful, and how they would prioritize the information. Below are some feedback for filtering the crime data.

"Ok so everything that would cause me physical harm is #1. Honestly, in no particular order because homicide, disorderly conduct, Etc are all horrible. Next, is anything that would cause damage to my property like car theft. Last, is things I just wouldn't want to see like prostitution." - Wellesley College alum, female, POC
"So I prioritize homicide/manslaughter/assault incidents, then might take measures to minimize risk of theft. I dont generally stay overnight in Boston so domestic issues rarely touch my radar." - Wellesley College senior, female, POC
"I would avoid all of these, in this order: Homicide, Manslaughter, Indecent Assault, Arson, Operating under the influence, Motor Vehicle Accident, Home Invasion, Missing Person reported, Robbery, Auto Theft, Property Damage, Disorderly conduct, Prostitution, Offences against child/family, Simple assault, Firearm violation, Larceny/Larceny from motor vehicles, Firearm discovery, Verbal disputes.
I put crimes that cause immediate bodily harm/casualties near the top, because you can buy a new car but a life is irreplaceable. Things after Missing Person are pretty random tbh lol just went with my gut." - Babson College senior, male, POC
